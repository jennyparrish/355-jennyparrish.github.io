Rhodies

Washington State's Pacific Rhododendron

Classified in the same genus as azaleas, Rhododendron macrophyllum (meaning "rose tree with big leaves") are also known by the names Coast Rhododendrons and California Rhododendrons, depending on who you ask and where you're seeing them.

This species of rhododendron (and there are over 1000) is a wide-leafed evergreen shrub with fairly shallow roots. It has flowers that bloom in clusters resembling pink pom-poms near the end of every spring, as if cheering for the birds and bugs that come to visit them.

Washington's beloved "Rhodies" are one of only five native rhododendron species in the state. They can be found growing wild west of the Cascades crest, from the coast of central California, all the way up to British Columbia. Often found around 1 to 5 meters in height (3-15ft), they have been known to grow as much as 30 feet tall. When they're forced to stretch higher to access sunlight that is blocked by their towering neighbors, the evergreen perennials, Rhodies will actually grow taller in the shade. They're happiest among the lowland rain-forest conifers of the Pacific Northwest, prefering damp soil over dry. They're also sturdy, like most things in the PNW, and are capable of thriving even along roadsides.

These flowers are a favorite dining spot of bumblebees, other bees, flies, beetles, moths, and hummingbirds; but, they're toxic to many mammals. People and livestock have gotten sick eating honey from bees who source their pollen from Rhodies. The substance came to be known as "mad honey," thanks to the toxin that is produced in all parts of the plant.
