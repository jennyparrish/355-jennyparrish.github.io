Rhodies

Washington State's Pacific Rhododendron

This species of rhododendron (and there are over 1000) is a wide-leafed evergreen shrub. These shrubs have flowers that bloom in clusters resembling pink pom-poms near the end of every spring; as if cheering for the birds and bugs that come to visit them. Being in the same genus as azaleas, the Rhododendron macrophyllum, which is literally translated to "rose tree with big leaves," are also known as Coast Rhododendrons and California Rhododendrons, depending on who you ask where ever you're seeing them.

Washington's beloved "Rhodies" are one of only five native rhododendron species in the state.

They can be found growing wild west of the Cascades crest.

From the coast of central California, all the way up to northern British Columbia.

Often found around 1-5 meters in height (3-15ft), they have been known to grow as high as 30 feet tall.

Being forced to stretch higher to reach sunlight that is so often blocked out by their towering neighbors, the

evergreen perennials

Rhodies actually grow taller in the shade.

They're happiest among the lowland rain-forest conifers
of the Pacific Northwest, prefering damp soil over dry.

They're also sturdy, like almost everything in the PNW,
and are capable of thriving even along roadsides.

Rhodies are siblings of azaleas.

They are a favorite food of bumblebees, bees, flies, beetles, moths, and hummingbirds; yet, they're toxic to many mammals.

People and livestock have gotten sick eating honey from bees who source their pollen from Rhodies.

The honey came to be known as "mad honey" due to the toxin that is produced in every part of the plant.
